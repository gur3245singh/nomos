## Problem

Suppose $S$ is a nonempty set of positive integers with the property that if $n$ is in $S$, then every positive divisor of
\[
2025^{n} - 15^{n}
\]
is in $S$.

Must $S$ contain all positive integers?

## Submission

To determine whether the set $ S $ must contain all positive integers, we analyze the condition given:

> If $ n \in S $, then every positive divisor of $ 2025^n - 15^n $ is also in $ S $.

---

### Step 1: Establishing that $ 1 \in S $

Since $ S $ is nonempty and contains at least one positive integer $ n $, and since $ 1 $ divides any positive integer (including $ 2025^n - 15^n $), it follows that $ 1 \in S $. This is a crucial starting point.

---

### Step 2: Factoring the Expression

We simplify:
$$
2025 = 15^2 \cdot 9 = 3^4 \cdot 5^2, \quad 15 = 3 \cdot 5.
$$
So,
$$
2025^n - 15^n = (3^4 \cdot 5^2)^n - (3 \cdot 5)^n = 3^{4n}5^{2n} - 3^n5^n = 3^n5^n(3^{3n}5^n - 1).
$$
Thus,
$$
2025^n - 15^n = 15^n (135^n - 1),
$$
where $ 135 = 3^3 \cdot 5 $.

This factorization is key. It tells us that the prime factors of $ 2025^n - 15^n $ are:

- The primes 3 and 5 from $ 15^n $,
- And the prime factors of $ 135^n - 1 $, which are **not** 3 or 5 (since $ 135 \equiv 0 \mod 3, 5 $).

---

### Step 3: Using Induction to Show All Positive Integers Are in $ S $

We proceed by **strong induction** on $ m \in \mathbb{Z}^+ $, showing that $ m \in S $ for all $ m \geq 1 $.

#### **Base Case**: $ m = 1 $

As established earlier, $ 1 \in S $.

#### **Inductive Hypothesis**

Assume for all $ k < m $, $ k \in S $.

#### **Inductive Step**

Let $ m > 1 $. We want to show $ m \in S $.

Write $ m = 3^a \cdot 5^b \cdot m' $, where $ \gcd(m', 15) = 1 $. Then:

- To have $ 3^a \mid 2025^n - 15^n $, we need $ n \geq a $ (from the valuation $ v_3(2025^n - 15^n) = n $).
- Similarly, $ 5^b \mid 2025^n - 15^n $ requires $ n \geq b $.
- For $ m' $, since $ \gcd(m', 15) = 1 $, we can write $ m' \mid 135^n - 1 $ if $ n $ is a multiple of the order of 135 modulo each prime power dividing $ m' $.

Let $ L $ be the least common multiple of the orders of 135 modulo the prime powers dividing $ m' $. Then $ m' \mid 135^n - 1 $ if $ n $ is a multiple of $ L $.

Now define:
$$
n = \max(a, b, L).
$$
Then:

- $ n \geq a $ and $ n \geq b $, so $ 3^a \mid 2025^n - 15^n $ and $ 5^b \mid 2025^n - 15^n $,
- $ n $ is a multiple of $ L $, so $ m' \mid 135^n - 1 $, hence $ m' \mid 2025^n - 15^n $,
- Since $ 3^a, 5^b, m' $ are pairwise coprime, their product $ m = 3^a \cdot 5^b \cdot m' \mid 2025^n - 15^n $.

Now, we need to verify that $ n < m $, so that by the inductive hypothesis, $ n \in S $, and thus $ m \in S $.

This is true because:

- For $ m' \geq 2 $, the order of 135 modulo $ m' $ is less than $ m' $ (by properties of modular orders),
- For $ a \geq 1 $, $ 3^a > a $,
- For $ b \geq 1 $, $ 5^b > b $,
- So $ n = \max(a, b, L) < m $.

Therefore, $ n \in S $, and since $ m \mid 2025^n - 15^n $, it follows that $ m \in S $.

---

### Step 4: Conclusion

By strong induction, **every positive integer** $ m $ satisfies $ m \in S $. Thus, $ S $ must contain **all** positive integers.

---

### Final Answer

$$
\boxed{\text{Yes}}
$$
