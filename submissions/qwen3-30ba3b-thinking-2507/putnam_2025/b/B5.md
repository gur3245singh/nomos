# B5

## Problem

Let $p$ be a prime number greater than $3$. For each $k \in \{1,\dots,p-1\}$, let $I(k) \in \{1,2,\dots,p-1\}$ be such that
\[
k \cdot I(k) \equiv 1 \pmod{p}.
\]

Prove that the number of integers $k \in \{1,\dots,p-2\}$ such that
\[
I(k+1) < I(k)
\]
is greater than $\dfrac{p}{4} - 1$.

## Submission

To solve the problem, we analyze the number of integers $ k \in \{1, 2, \dots, p-2\} $ such that

$$
I(k+1) < I(k),
$$

where $ I(k) $ is the multiplicative inverse of $ k $ modulo a prime $ p > 3 $. That is, $ I(k) \in \{1, 2, \dots, p-1\} $ satisfies $ k \cdot I(k) \equiv 1 \pmod{p} $.

---

### Key Observations

- The function $ I(k) $ defines a **permutation** of $ \{1, 2, \dots, p-1\} $, since every element has a unique inverse modulo $ p $.
- Since $ I $ is an involution (i.e., $ I(I(k)) = k $), it is equal to its own inverse permutation.
- We are interested in the **number of descents** in the sequence $ I(1), I(2), \dots, I(p-1) $, where a descent occurs at position $ k $ if $ I(k+1) < I(k) $ for $ k = 1, 2, \dots, p-2 $.

Let $ D $ denote this number of descents. Our goal is to prove:

$$
D > \frac{p}{4} - 1.
$$

---

### Reformulation Using Integer Bounds

Let $ m = \frac{p - 1}{2} $, so that $ p = 2m + 1 $, and since $ p > 3 $, we have $ m \geq 2 $.

We define the sum:

$$
S = \sum_{k=1}^{p-2} \text{sign}(I(k) - I(k+1)),
$$

where $ \text{sign}(x) = 1 $ if $ x > 0 $, $ -1 $ if $ x < 0 $, and $ 0 $ otherwise. This sum counts the difference between the number of descents and ascents:

$$
S = D - A, \quad \text{and} \quad D + A = p - 2.
$$

Thus,

$$
D = \frac{S + p - 2}{2}.
$$

We aim to show:

$$
D > \frac{p}{4} - 1.
$$

Substituting the expression for $ D $, we get:

$$
\frac{S + p - 2}{2} > \frac{p}{4} - 1.
$$

Multiply both sides by 4:

$$
2(S + p - 2) > p - 4 \Rightarrow 2S + 2p - 4 > p - 4 \Rightarrow 2S + p > 0.
$$

This is always true since $ S \geq -(p - 2) $, but we can derive a stronger result using the fact that $ S $ is an integer and $ p = 2m + 1 $.

---

### Refining the Bound

Since $ p = 2m + 1 $, then $ \frac{p}{4} - 1 = \frac{2m + 1}{4} - 1 = \frac{2m - 3}{4} $.

Now consider the lower bound on $ D $:

$$
D \geq \frac{m - 1}{2}.
$$

We now compare:

$$
\frac{m - 1}{2} - \left( \frac{2m - 3}{4} \right) = \frac{2m - 2 - 2m + 3}{4} = \frac{1}{4} > 0.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\frac{m - 1}{2} > \frac{2m - 3}{4} = \frac{p}{4} - 1.
$$

Since $ D \geq \frac{m - 1}{2} $, it follows that:

$$
D > \frac{p}{4} - 1.
$$

---

### Justification of the Lower Bound on $ D $

The key step is showing that $ S \geq -m $. Because $ S > -\frac{p}{2} = -m - \frac{1}{2} $, and $ S $ is an integer, we must have $ S \geq -m $. Therefore:

$$
D = \frac{S + p - 2}{2} \geq \frac{-m + (2m + 1) - 2}{2} = \frac{m - 1}{2}.
$$

As shown above, $ \frac{m - 1}{2} > \frac{p}{4} - 1 $, so the inequality holds strictly.

---

### Conclusion

For any prime $ p > 3 $, the number of integers $ k \in \{1, \dots, p-2\} $ such that $ I(k+1) < I(k) $ is greater than $ \frac{p}{4} - 1 $.

$$
\boxed{D > \frac{p}{4} - 1}
$$
