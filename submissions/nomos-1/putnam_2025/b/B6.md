# B6

## Problem

Let $\mathbb{N} = \{1,2,3,\dots\}$. Find the largest real constant $r$ such that there exists a function
\[
g : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}
\]
satisfying
\[
g(n+1) - g(n) \ge (g(g(n)))^{r}
\]
for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$.

## Submission

**The largest real constant \(r\) for which such a function exists is \(\boxed{0}\).**

**Proof.**  
Let \(g:\mathbb{N}\to\mathbb{N}\) satisfy  

\[
g(n+1)-g(n)\ge (g(g(n)))^{r}\qquad\text{for all }n\in\mathbb{N}.
\]

---

**1.  \(g\) is strictly increasing.**  
Because \(g(g(n))\in\mathbb{N}\), \((g(g(n)))^{r}>0\); therefore \(g(n+1)-g(n)>0\) and \(g(n+1)>g(n)\).

**2.  \(g(n)\ge n\) for every \(n\).**  
\(g(1)\ge1\).  Assume \(g(k)\ge k\).  Since \(g(k+1)>g(k)\) and both are integers, \(g(k+1)\ge g(k)+1\ge k+1\).  Induction gives \(g(n)\ge n\).

**3.  \(g(n)\le n\) for every \(n\).**  
Suppose there exists \(n\) with \(g(n)>n\).  Then \(g(n)\ge n+1\) (integers).  Because \(g\) is increasing,  
\(g(g(n))\ge g(n+1)\).  The inequality then yields  

\[
g(n+1)-g(n)\ge g(g(n))\ge g(n+1)\;\Longrightarrow\;-g(n)\ge0,
\]

which contradicts \(g(n)\ge1\).  Hence \(g(n)\le n\).

**4.  From 2 and 3 we obtain \(g(n)=n\) for all \(n\); i.e. \(g\) must be the identity function.**

**5.  Substituting \(g(n)=n\) into the original inequality gives \(1\ge n^{r}\) for all \(n\in\mathbb{N}\).**  
For \(n=2\) this forces \(r\le0\); for \(n=1\) it holds for any \(r\).  Thus a necessary condition is \(r\le0\).

**6.  For any \(r\le0\) the identity function \(g(n)=n\) works:**  
\(g(n+1)-g(n)=1\) and \(n^{r}\le1\) (since \(n\ge1\) and \(r\le0\)).  Hence a function exists exactly for \(r\le0\).

Consequently the largest admissible constant is \(r=0\). \(\square\)
